If two systems, $A$ and $B$ are separately in equilibrium with a third system $C$, then they are also in equilibrium with one another.

The zeroth law leads to implying the existence of empirical temperature $\Theta$, so the systems in equilibrium are at the same temperature.

The equilibrium of systems $A$, $B$, and $C$ can be described by sets of coordinates $(A_1, A_2,...)$, $(B_1, B_2,...)$, and $(C_1, C_2,...)$. The assumption that $A$ and $C$ are in equilibrium results in the constraint

$$
f_{AC}(A_1,A_2,...,C_1,C_2,...)=0.
\tag{1}
$$

The equilibrium of $B$ and $C$ also implies a similar constraint

$$
f_{BC}(B_1,B_2,...,C_1,C_2,...)=0.
\tag{2}
$$

These two equations can be used to derive

$$
F_{AC}(A_1,A_2,...,C_2,...)=C_1=F_{BC}(B_1,B_2,...,C_2,...).
\tag3
$$

However, now the functions $F_{AC}$ and $F_{BC}$ still depend on the coordinates of $(C_2,C_3,...)$. Note that according to the zeroth law, $A$ and $B$  are also in equilibrium, so we have

$$
f_{AB}(A_1,A_2,...,B_1,B_2,...)=0.
\tag4
$$

Therefore, this equation should not depend on which system $C$ is used as the intermediate system. Although the equation $(3)$ appears to contain $(C_2,C_3,...)$, this equality should hold independently of the particular state of $C$. In other words, $C$ is only an intermediate system.

Because of this, we are allowed to fix an arbitrary state $(C_2,C_3,...)=(c_2,c_3,...)$, and define

$$
\Theta_{A}(A_1,A_2,...)=F_{AC}(A_1,A_2,...,c_2,c_3,...),
\tag5
$$
$$
\Theta_{B}(B_1,B_2,...)=F_{BC}(B_1,B_2,...,c_2,c_3,...).
\tag6
$$

Then the equilibrium between $A$ and $B$ becomes

$$
\Theta_{A}(A_1,A_2,...)=\Theta_{B}(B_1,B_2,...).
\tag7
$$

As a result, the equilibrium is characterized by a function $\Theta$ of thermodynamic coordinates.
